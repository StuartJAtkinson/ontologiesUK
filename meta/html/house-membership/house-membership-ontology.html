House membership ontology

Classes | Object properties | Data properties | Subclasses | Namespaces |
A model describing a person occupying a seat in a House, by means of an incumbency over a period of time.

Classes

# Constituency group class

A group of people represented by a person who has won an election, including people other than those eligible to vote at the time of election.

# Disqualification class

Disqualification is the period of time for which a Member was disqualified from sitting or voting in a House.

Superclass: SeatIncumbencyInterruption.

# Disqualification type class

The type of disqualification of a disqualified Member, for example: in the House of Commons, on the grounds of being a Peer or Lord Spiritual in the House of Lords, or being convicted of treason under the Forfeiture Act 1870 - in the House of Lords, on the grounds of holding judicial office, being a Member of the European Parliament, or being bankrupt.

# End reason class

A reason for the ending of a seat incumbency, for example: a Member may leave a House following an election loss, by standing down, by retirement, or death.

# House class

A parliamentary House, for example: the House of Lords.

# House seat class

A seat as a position in a House - rather than a physical seat or constituency - which may be allocated to a Member, for example: a seat in the House of Commons associated with a constituency - or a seat in the House of Lords allocated to the Archbishop of Canterbury, inherited by a hereditary peer or granted to a life peer.

# House seat type class

The type of a House seat, for example: seats in the House of Commons allocated by means of the first past the post electoral system, seats in the House of Lords allocated to hereditary peers, life peers, excepted hereditary peers or Lords Spiritual. The Lords Spiritual are a group of the bishops of five named dioceses as ex-officio Members, together with the 21 longest serving English diocesan bishops not in that group - eligibility for which includes criteria set out in the Lords Spiritual (Women) Act 2015.

# Leave of absence class

A period during which a Member of the House of Lords is authorised to not attend sittings.

Superclass: SeatIncumbencyInterruption.

# Parliament period class

The time period of a single Parliament between the date of its first assembly after a general election and its dissolution. A Parliament period contains contiguous session and prorogation periods: during a parliament period Parliament is either in session or prorogued. Until 2015, the dissolution proclamation named the date for Parliament to reassemble. After 2015, a Parliament begins on the date named in the proclamation issued after a dissolution. The start date of a Parliament may be changed by subsequent proclamations. A Parliament ends at the next dissolution. Until 2015 the date of dissolution was indeterminate and fixed by proclamation. After 2015 the date of dissolution is determined by the Fixed Term Parliament Act 2011, or is named in a proclamation after Parliament has voted for an early general election. Parliament periods are the same across both Houses. Parliaments are numbered ordinally from the first Parliament of the United Kingdom, sitting in 1801.

# Person class

A person.

# Seat incumbency class

The occupation of a seat in a House by a person, for a period of time. A person may have more than one incumbency over time. Only one person can be incumbent in a house seat at one time. A person having a seat incumbency which has a start date in the past and an end date in the future - or which has no end date - is a current Member. A person having a seat incumbency with an end date in the past - and no current incumbency - is an ex-Member.

# Seat incumbency interruption class

A period of interruption to a seat incumbency, for example: by suspension, leave of absence or disqualification.

# Suspension class

A period of exclusion of a Member during which the Member is not authorised to sit in a House.

Superclass: SeatIncumbencyInterruption.

Swearing in order data property

The order in which Members take an oath or make an affirmation at the beginning of a new Parliament or following a by-election. Swearing in order may be used in determining the longest serving member of a House, known as the Father or Mother of the House.
