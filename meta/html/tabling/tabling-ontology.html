Tabling ontology

Classes · Object properties · Data properties · Subclasses · Namespaces · Depiction
A model to describe the activity of members tabling parliamentary questions, motions, amendments and nominations.

Classes

House

Class

A parliamentary House e.g. the House of Lords.

Seat incumbency

Class

The occupation of a seat in a House by a person, for a period of time. A person may have more than one incumbency over time. Only one person can be incumbent in a house seat at one time. A person having a seat incumbency which has a start date in the past and an end date in the future - or which has no end date - is a current Member. A person having a seat incumbency with an end date in the past - and no current incumbency - is an ex-Member.

Session

Class

The period during which Parliament is convened. The first session of a parliament begins on the same day as the parliament period: this date is given in the dissolution proclamation at the end of the previous parliament. The session ends on the day set in a subsequent prorogation proclamation. Subsequent sessions within the parliament period begin and end on dates set in subsequent prorogation proclamations. The last session of a parliament ends with prorogation followed by dissolution. Session periods are the same for both Houses.

Tabled thing

Class

Any item of business which has been tabled e.g. a motion, parliamentary question, amendment or committee membership nomination. Note not all things that can be tabled must be tabled e.g. manuscript amendments are amendments which are raised on the day the business occurs. In other words, no notice has been given. For this reason they do not appear on the day's business papers and are rarely selected for debate. Manuscript amendments do not appear on the marshalled list.

Tabling

Class

The act of tabling a business item into either the House of Commons or House of Lords. Tabling is the act of formally putting forward a question, a motion, an amendment or a committee membership nomination. Members do this by submitting it to procedural clerks. The item will then appear in the next day's business papers as a written notice. Each House has a Table Office where parliamentary questions, motions for debate and, in the Commons, early day motions are tabled. Amendments to bills are always tabled in the Public Bill Offices of each House.
